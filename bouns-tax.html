Bonus Tax Rate 2022: How Are Bonuses Taxed?

Bonuses are monetary payments, monetary-equivalent rewards, and non-monetary perks, such as additional paid vacation days. In any case, you’ll need to know how much you’re taking home. We’ll cover how bonuses are taxed so you aren’t blindsided come April.

Nine states have no income tax.

But employees in all states must pay taxes on bonuses.

How much? How often? And exactly how are bonuses taxed?

Read on to find out more about the current bonus tax rate.

What Are Bonuses?

Bonuses are monies paid, or privileges given, to employees in addition to their usual salary or hourly wages.

Sometimes bonuses are part of special employee incentivization programs or from unexpected market profit increases. Often, they’re an expected Christmas gift or the result of a successful referral.

There are two basic types of bonuses:

  • Discretionary: These are not guaranteed and can tie to individual or corporate performance.
  • Nondiscretionary: These are expected and explicated in an employee’s benefits package.

Specific types of bonuses include:

  • Profit-sharing bonuses. Employees get a certain share of the company’s quarterly profits, allowing them a sense of ownership in the company.
  • Gainsharing bonuses. This is tied to metrics: High-performing employees take home additional money based on the increased productivity they’ve provided their company.
  • Spot bonuses. Small bonuses for various things — working late, and making deadlines — are utilized to incentivize employees.
  • Non-cash bonuses. These come in the form of special perks — an extra paid day off, for instance.
  • Referral bonuses. If a new employee is hired based on a current employee’s recommendation, that current employee gets a finder’s fee.
  • Holiday bonuses.
  • Commission. A commission is a sort of bonus. These are more common in retail and offered to encourage employee productivity and expertise.

How Are Bonuses Taxed?

Bonuses aren’t taxed as regular income, which depending on the standard income tax rate where you are may not be great news.

Instead, bonuses are labeled “supplemental wages,” which the IRS taxes at a flat 22% withholding rate.

What constitutes supplemental wages?

  • Accumulated sick leave
  • Back pay
  • Certain commissions
  • Overtime pay
  • Payments for nondeductible moving expenses
  • Prizes and awards
  • Reported tips
  • Restricted stock units
  • Retroactive pay increase
  • Severance pay
  • Sign-on bonuses

More or less everything other than standard wages and stock.

Understanding the Bonus Tax Rate

If your bonus is an achievement award, you won’t have to pay bonus tax on it, so long as:

  • The award isn’t given in cash, gift cards, money orders, vacations, entertainment tickets of various kinds, stocks or bonds, or other similar cash-value items
  • The award is in the form of personal property
  • The total value of the award is less than $1,600

But in other cases, expect bonuses to be taxed as supplemental income.

Tax Withholding on Bonuses

Employers calculate your withholding in one of two ways:

  • The percentage method. Also known as the flat rate method, this is the most straightforward way for employers to do this. In the short term, it results in more take-home pay for you. Taxed at the usual 22%, unless your bonuses total $1 million or more, at which point it rises to 37%.
  • The aggregate method. Withholdings of bonuses added to regular income are taxed at your regular, tax bracket-specific income rate. This means a larger chunk of your bonus is taken out up front, but that can pay off in April: You’re less likely to owe the IRS money on that bonus — never a welcome surprise.

So as with anything, there are risks and benefits involved in each choice.

In either case, LinkedIn blogger Daniel Watkins reminds readers: “Don’t be fooled by the larger percent of gross earnings withheld in the one-time payout. It is important to understand the full year impact of both scenarios.”

In other words, be patient: Larger withholdings can pay off later.

Simplifying the Bonus Tax Rate

Think of it this way to simplify: Your bonus is generally taxed at 22%. Relatively few employers will use the aggregate method because it is labor-intensive for them. Just be prepared to find out you owe a bit extra having used the percentage method.

And if you’re getting performance-based bonuses, keep at it. High-performing employees are the ones earmarked for raises and promotions.

Key Takeaways:

  • Bonuses are supplemental income, non-monetary perks, and other irregular rewards employees receive on a scheduled basis or during non-recurring scenarios.
  • Bonus pay is considered supplemental income and is taxed at the federal fixed rate of 22% for bonuses under $1 million.
